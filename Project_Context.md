Second Project – Description, Grading, and Deliverables
Grading Criteria
The second project has a weight of 20% of your course final grade, with two sprints you will have to work on as a team: each costing 10% of your course final grade. The project and each of its sprints will be graded using the format below, based on two core factors: product and process.
Concerning your product, your team will need to satisfy their client (a member from the instructional team: i.e., your mentor TA) based on the user stories you all agreed to complete for the sprint. User stories will be graded as all-or-nothing: partial functionality will not earn you points. User stories must have been deployed on PythonAnywhere and work successfully to count for functionality.
Concerning your process, your team will need to follow Scrum and its different elements. Communication, cooperation, planning, feedback, and balance are essential. Issues that will sanction your team are:
o Lack of effective communication with the client.
o Lack of effective communication among teammates.
o Lack of planning and follow-ups (daily standups).
o Unbalanced distribution of work.
o Lack of cooperation and conflict resolution.
Per our syllabus, all communication among teams, clients, and members, must be via our institutional channels: Microsoft Teams, Canvas, Ed Discussion, and GT Outlook.
At the end of each sprint (12% of your course final grade) of the project, based on these criteria, your grade will be calculated as follows:
Outstanding (100% of the grade): Both product and process were successful!
a. Product: Your app successfully works, features all user stories required, and presents an appealing GUI that aligns with the stated design expectations (see Design Guidelines section).
AND
b. Process: The process was successful and incorporated effective communication, cooperation, planning, feedback, and balance. If issues happened, they were reported proactively and found resolution before the end of the sprint. Additionally, all process deliverables (sprint planning and sprint retrospective) were successfully done and submitted on time.
Okay (70% of the grade)—When at least one of the following is true:
a. Product: Your app features less user stories than those required.
b. Product: Your app has flaws in its GUI design.
c. Process: Two process deliverables (sprint planning and sprint retrospective) were not submitted on time.
d. Process: One process deliverable (sprint planning and sprint retrospective) was not successfully completed.
e. Process: There was a lack of effective communication with the client.
f. Process: There was a lack of effective communication among teammates.
g. Process: There were little to no missed daily standups.
h. Process: There was an unbalanced distribution of work.
Less than Okay (50% of the grade)—When two to four of the following are true:
a. Product: Your app features less user stories than those required.
b. Product: Your app has flaws in its GUI design.
c. Process: Two process deliverables (sprint planning and sprint retrospective) were not submitted on time.
d. Process: One process deliverable (sprint planning and sprint retrospective) was not successfully completed.
e. Process: There was a lack of effective communication with the client.
f. Process: There was a lack of effective communication among teammates.
g. Process: There were little to no missed daily standups.
h. Process: There was an unbalanced distribution of work.
Rejected (0% of the grade)—When five or more of the following are true:
a. Product: Your app features less user stories than those required.
b. Product: Your app has flaws in its GUI design.
c. Process: Two process deliverables (sprint planning and sprint retrospective) were not submitted on time.
d. Process: One process deliverable (sprint planning and sprint retrospective) was not successfully completed.
e. Process: There was a lack of effective communication with the client.
f. Process: There was a lack of effective communication among teammates.
g. Process: There were little to no missed daily standups.
h. Process: There was an unbalanced distribution of work.
Disclaimer #1: Teams are required to record teammates' contributions and to effectively communicate with their mentor TA. Not communicating issues early may lead the team to be graded below "Outstanding."

Disclaimer #7: In addition to your project deliverables for each sprint, your team will complete two supporting documents per sprint: (1) a sprint planning document and (2) a sprint retrospective document. To apply for and protect your individual sprint grade in the case of one or more of your teammates slacking off:
Communicate proactively and professionally via email or Teams with your mentor TA, requesting the issuing of a formal strike. Elaborate on your rationale and the reasons behind your request, and provide an explanation of what you have done and what they have been missing.
Two scenarios to consider from here:
In the case that it is the first strike, your mentor and other members of our instructional team will help resolve the issue and follow up closely with your case. At this point, the sprint's grade will still be considered team-based, based on product and process. The first strike does not lead to a grade penalty.
In the case of a second or further strike, penalties will be applied to individual students who are slacking in their contributions. The sprint's grade will be adjusted accordingly, based on individual contributions to the project's product and process. That means someone who completes their personal user stories will be assessed solely on those stories, with no penalty from slacking teammates.
Sprint Demo
To ensure that teams understand and can explain the code produced, each sprint will include a practical, assessment-oriented live demo. During the demo, teams may lose points from their initial product and process score; that is, students may complete all user stories and demonstrate a good process, but still not receive a perfect score if the team underperforms on the demo.
During the demo, the client TA may ask students to explain aspects of their project, including (but not limited to):
Completed user stories
Code structure and organization
Design and architectural decisions
Individual contributions and understanding
Each sprint demo will assess five competency areas. For each competency area that is not satisfactorily demonstrated, up to 3 points may be deducted from the team's sprint grade.
That is,
Final sprint grade = (Product & process grade) - ((# of competency areas unsatisfactorily completed) * 3)
Grading Examples
Team 7 receives a 100 for their product and process grade for sprint 1. During the demo, they satisfactorily demonstrate 4 out of 5 competency areas.
→ Final Sprint 1 grade: 97
Team 2 receives a 100 for their product and process grade for sprint 1 and satisfactorily demonstrates all 5 competency areas.
→ Final Sprint 1 grade: 100
Team 1 receives a 70 for their product and process grade for sprint 1. During the demo, they satisfactorily demonstrate 3 out of 5 competency areas.
→ Final Sprint 1 grade: 64
Strike System
The strike system is a way for the instructional team to ensure contribution fairness and individual accountability. Individuals who have received a strike may have the strike lifted.
Our strike system entails:
Strike 1: There is a written warning via email from the client TA, CCing your instructor and head TA. This first warning does not lead to a grade penalty.
Strike 2: There is a written warning via email from the client TA, CCing your instructor and head TA. This second warning leads to a grade deduction of 30 points (out of 100 points) on the project's sprint. We then apply what's described in Disclaimer #4.
Strike 3: There is a written warning via email from the client TA, CCing your instructor and head TA. This third and final warning leads to a grade deduction of 100 points (out of 100 points) on the project's sprint.
What behavior will warrant a strike?
A strike may be issued for, but is not limited to, the following behaviors:
Repeated failure to complete assigned user stories or sprint responsibilities
Missing or consistently disengaging from team meetings, standups, or sprint reviews
Lack of meaningful contribution to the project's product or process
Poor or unprofessional communication with teammates or the client TA
Ignoring feedback or action items provided by the instructional team
Claiming work that was not completed or was completed by another team member
Repeatedly submitting low-effort or non-functional work that hinders team progress
Strikes are not issued for honest mistakes, learning struggles, or temporary difficulties when proactively and professionally communicated to the mentor TA.
Can I get a strike lifted?
Yes. A strike may be lifted at the discretion of the instructional team if the student:
Communicates proactively with their mentor TA
Demonstrates clear improvement in contribution and engagement
Completes any remediation tasks assigned by the instructional team
Follows through on agreed-upon responsibilities in subsequent sprint work
Strike removal is not automatic and will be evaluated on a case-by-case basis based on documented behavior and improvement.

Design Guidelines
Ultimately, you will create a user-facing web application for a client; for this to be successful, your application must adhere to the following requirements:
Client TA preferences. You are designing for a client. Design decisions (layout, navigation, feature placement, and overall experience) should align with feedback and expectations communicated by your mentor TA. Your team is responsible to request and respond to your client's feedback.
Coherent brand. Across your website, you should demonstrate a clear and consistent brand identity. Users should feel they are interacting with a single, cohesive product—not a collection of disconnected pages. This includes:
o Consistent color palette
o Consistent typography
o Reusable UI components (buttons, cards, headers, etc.)
o Consistent tone and visual language across pages
Disclaimer: We strongly encourage that you all use good UI design practices. Please refer to this reference about UI Design principles: reference.
Theme alignment. While all teams will be designing for the same set of user stories, there is still creative flexibility in how those stories are presented through a chosen theme. For example, an application could adopt a water-based theme; instead of a page titled "Trending," users might see a page called "Where's the tide going?" that conveys the same functionality.
There are a variety of excellent design inspiration resources online that you can use including Figma Community, Material Design (Google), Dribbble, NN/g (Nielsen Norman Group). Please note that these are meant to be used as inspiration, not sources to copy from.

Sprint Summary Videos
At the end of each sprint, teams are required to create and submit a sprint summary of video. These videos are intended to mirror industry-style sprint reviews, where teams verbally communicate progress, technical decisions, and challenges to stakeholders.
Sprint summary videos emphasize:
Clear technical communication
Shared ownership of the project
Reflection on both product and process
Individual accountability and balanced participation
These videos allow the instructional team to directly assess each team member's understanding of the system, their contributions, and their ability to explain design decisions—skills that are critical in real-world software development.
Required Video Content
What was completed in this sprint?
a. Which user stories were implemented
b. A brief demonstration of working functionality (on the deployed website)
How was the work completed?
a. Key technical decisions (architecture, tools, libraries, APIs)
b. How responsibilities were divided among team members
c. How Scrum practices were applied (planning, standups, reviews)
What challenges did the team encounter and how were they resolved?
a. Technical or design challenges
b. Process or coordination issues
What is next?
a. Remaining work or known limitations
b. Planned focus for the next sprint

Context Description
Early-career job seekers often face challenges finding roles that match their skills, interests, and location preferences. At the same time, recruiters struggle to identify and evaluate applicants who are a good fit for their openings, especially when managing large pools of candidates.
Your team has been asked to design and build a web application that bridges this gap. The platform should serve as a meeting point between young professionals searching for opportunities and recruiters looking for talent. It should support creating and managing professional profiles, posting and exploring job opportunities, and enabling recruiters to search, track, and engage with applicants.
Because location plays a key role in employment, the platform should also integrate map-based features to help job seekers visualize opportunities geographically and recruiters understand applicant distribution.
This project is intended to challenge you to think about multiple perspectives (considering Job seekers, Recruiters, and Administrators) while practicing core software engineering skills such as requirements analysis, system design, team collaboration, and web development.
User Stories
As a Job Seeker, I want to create a profile with my headline, skills, education, work experience, and links so recruiters can learn about me.
As a Job Seeker, I want to search for jobs with filters (title, skills, location, salary range, remote/on-site, visa sponsorship) so I can find opportunities that match my needs.
As a Job Seeker, I want to apply to a job with one click and include a tailored note so my application feels personalized.
As a Job Seeker, I want to track the status of my applications (Applied → Review → Interview → Offer → Closed) so I know where I stand.
As a Job Seeker, I want to set privacy options on my profile so I control what recruiters can see.
As a Job Seeker, I want to receive recommendations for jobs based on my skills so I discover opportunities I might have missed.
As a Job Seeker, I want to view job postings on an interactive map so I can see which ones are near me.
As a Job Seeker, I want to filter jobs on the map by distance from my current location so I can prioritize nearby opportunities.
As a Job Seeker, I want to set a preferred commute radius (e.g., 10 miles) on the map so I only see jobs within a reasonable travel distance.
As a Recruiter, I want to post and edit job roles so candidates can apply to my openings.
As a Recruiter, I want to search for candidates by skills, location, and projects so I can find talent that fits my positions.
As a Recruiter, I want to organize applicants in a pipeline (e.g., a Kanban board) so I can easily manage hiring stages.
As a Recruiter, I want to message candidates inside the platform so I can contact them without the use of personal emails.
As a Recruiter, I want to email candidates through the platform so I can reach out to them through their personal emails.
As a Recruiter, I want to save a candidate search and get notified about new matches so I don't have to repeat the same queries.
As a Recruiter, I want to receive candidate recommendations for my job postings so I find qualified applicants faster.
As a Recruiter, I want to pin my job posting's office location on a map so candidates know exactly where the job is based.
As a Recruiter, I want to see clusters of applicants by location on a map so I understand where most candidates are coming from.
As an Administrator, I want to manage users and roles so the platform remains fair and safe.
As an Administrator, I want to moderate or remove job posts so the platform stays free of spam or abuse.
As an Administrator, I want to export data (CSV) for reporting purposes so stakeholders can analyze usage.
